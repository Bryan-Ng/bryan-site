You are wasting your time

May 7, 2020

Bryan Ng

"Lost time is never found again," Benjamin Franklin.

I (am trying to) live with sense of urgency.

Perspective.
The average lifespan is approximately 80 years. The first 20 years are spent in a daze, bumping into things, learning the rules of the world and how to react to your surroundings. The last 10-20 years may be spent in less than optimal physical and mental health. That gives us the 40 years between 20-60 (If we are lucky enough) to "live" . Further, time may feel relative. In the earlier years of oneâ€™s life, one year feels like an eternity. When you are 5, a year would equate to 1/5th of your time on earth. As you age, your relationship with time changes. One year becomes 1/20th of your time on earth. Days pass with increasing speed. The 40 years that you have to "live" become shorter and shorter.

Given this increasingly limited resource, why do we choose to spend our days as if we have no shortage of it?

Time limit.
Parkinson's law asserts that work expands to fill the time you give it. Given one week to finish an assignment, you will probably finish that assignment in one week. However, most people, like myself, give ourselves one day to finish assignments, and thus complete it in that timeframe. Productivity, can be boosted if you give yourself a time limit that can be achieved if you stretch towards it. In fact, some people may feel that the pressure of a deadline is critical for creativity, to focus on essentials, and to shipping your work. Beyond the application of this theory to work/school, you can see how it may be relevant to your personal life. In our daily lives, we may spend time more wisely if we provide a time limit to things such as grocery shopping (limiting your time wandering and buying things you don't need). In life in general, we can appreciate the limited nature of time. By recognizing that there is a limit to the time we spend on earth, we may adopt an urgency to spend our time better.

Make your work meaningful.
40 hours a week is a lot of time. If you are spending this much time per week doing something, you might as well make it something you are proud of. It's a shame to waste so much time doing something with a clock in clock out mentality. Try your best, strive to improve, and make your work meaningful.
Maybe this wasn't your dream job. Unfortunately, not everyone gets their dream job, and they have to live with that reality. If you truly believe that you do not and can not make your work meaningful in that position (e.g. you have a sense of dread for the work week to begin), find a position where you can.
Otherwise, we have the choice to make our job, whatever it is, something meaningful. Often, that makes us better at what we do, and in turn, makes work more enjoyable.

Leisure.
Make time for yourself and others. Schedule time to unwind and be human. It's easy to become obsessed with work and forget the relationships that matter to us. We are more creative, happier, and less likely to burn out when we schedule leisure time. There's probably a youtube video out there with an older individual regretting not spending more time doing the things they love with the people they love.

Time well spent.
Imagine yourself on your deathbed and consider: Did I live a good life? Why or why not? Would I go back and change anything?
You don't want to look back at my life and wonder where all the time went. Maybe we should measure success with units of "time well spent".

What's your value and audience?

April 19, 2020

Bryan Ng

I have been thinking about what "value" means recently:
Individuals are compensated, usually with money, in exchange for the services they provide society. Compensation depends on 1) Society's perspective of how valuable your service is (your value), and 2) How much of society benefits from your services (your audience).
It is helpful to think about value and audience through different professions.

The value of Cashiers, is to facilitate transactions between individuals. It's important, but you won't die without seeing a cashier. The audience is limited depending on the goods that are sold and the community they serve.
The value of Doctors, is to promote health and prevent death. Not dying is important to the individual, and having healthy individuals is important to society (to be productive). Everyone requires health care, however, the audience is limited to the number of patients the Doctor can see in their community.
The value of a Top Manager (e.g. CEO) of a large corporation, is, depending on the principles of the company, to provide valuable services/goods that allow society to run. Their audience is usually large, and serves multiple communities or countries.

The greater the value (V) and audience (A), the greater the compensation (C). V x A = C.
Even if what you are doing is extremely valuable, if you have no audience, you won't benefit society. 10 x 0 = 0.
If you are doing something that you think is valuable, you need to find an audience for it.

Why you should read

April 13, 2020

Bryan Ng

"I haven't read since high school."
"I don't have time to read."

1. You have the time to read, you just haven't taken the time to try.
Over the past two years I picked up reading again. I took a conscious effort to read 30 minutes every day, whether it be during my daily commute (I actually prefer public transit now because it forces me to read) or right before I go to bed. I argue 30 minutes is all the time we need to pick up reading again - we probably spend more time scrolling through Instagram, Twitter, or Facebook, skimming through things that we probably don't care about. I ended up reading 9 books in my first year, nothing crazy, but it was a big improvement from my usual (0/year).

2. Reading opens your mind.
Books will introduce to us new ideas, new ways to approach problems, or even challenge our own beliefs. I have a background in health sciences, but I found the most rewarding books to be about history, economics, and business. I think the beauty of reading is that it makes us realize how little we know about the world. In the movie "Inception", (Spoiler) the protagonist plants an idea into his wife's mind, which ends up changing her perception forever. Books introduce ideas that can change the way we understand the world.

3. Compound effect.
Reading 30 minutes every day adds up to 182.5 hours per year. I'm a slow reader and that led to 9 books. More importantly, we learn more about ourselves and the world with every book. These lessons add up in the long run. It's surprising how much you change by creating small, seemingly inconsequential, habits.

4. Empathy.
Books let us enter the mind of someone who lives a completely different life than we do. We learn what shapes other people's perspectives by learning their culture, their upbringing, and the challenges they've faced. We're often stuck in our own bubble and forget to take the effort to understand others. Reading reminds us of the diversity of mankind. We don't need to agree with other people's actions or ideas, but we can't disagree with them unless we really understand them. I think this is extremely important in today's society.

5. Learn from other's mistakes.
Most of the challenges we face have probably been solved before by others (others that are, let's face it, much smarter than we are). We can accelerate our chances of success by learning off of the mistakes others have made and their approach to overcoming similar problems. As such, I think there is a strong case to be made to study history. If we stop learning, we are doomed to repeat mistakes we've made in the past.
